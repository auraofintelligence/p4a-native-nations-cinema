The proposals / the Community-Hour

One hour is one hour.

Every economy on Earth runs on a hidden engine: care. Raising children, sitting with elders, tending Country, teaching language, showing up after the flood. The market ledger records none of it — then wonders why communities fray. The C-Hour is a proposal for making that work visible: a receipt for one verified hour of contribution, where an elder's hour, a coder's hour and a teenager's hour weigh exactly the same.

The instrument

A receipt, not a coin.

IsA verified record

One C-Hour records exactly one hour of verified human contribution to community wellbeing or ecological stewardship, on a transparent community ledger.

IsRadically equal

One hour of youth mentoring equals one hour of software engineering equals one hour of caring for an elder. No skill-weighting, no market pricing of persons. 1 hour = 1 C-Hour.

Is notA speculative asset

Non-tradeable on markets, non-yield-bearing, not pegged to any currency, never sold. Its value cannot go up, which is the point: it measures, it does not speculate.

Is notA wage or a score

It does not replace paid work, does not rank people, and must never become a surveillance instrument. What communities let it redeem — festival entry, transport, co-op access — is each community's own call.

The ethical spine

Mobilise the care economy. Never monetise it.

These two words are the whole design, and they are not interchangeable. Monetising care means applying market extraction to it: pricing it, packaging it, letting distant shareholders profit from the labour of holding a community together. Everyone has watched that movie.

Mobilising care means making it visible and resourced without surrendering it to the market: the contribution is counted, honoured and reciprocated inside the community that received it. The C-Hour is deliberately built so it cannot be monetised — no transferability to speculators, no yield, no exchange listing — because any door left open to extraction will eventually be walked through.

For native nations, this rhymes with something old: reciprocity economies, gift obligations and kinship duties ran sophisticated societies for tens of thousands of years without a stock ticker. The C-Hour is not an invention in that lineage — at best it is a receipt printer bolted onto it, and each nation would decide whether that bolt-on honours or insults the original. Both answers are respected here.

Under whose law?

Each nation defines its own hours.

Nothing about the C-Hour is meant to be universal except the arithmetic. What counts as contribution — caring for Country, ceremony support, language teaching, flood response, fixing the community truck — is defined by each community under its own law and protocol. A nation could adopt the pattern, rename it in its own language, restrict it to citizens, weight nothing, and refuse interoperability with anyone. That is not a failure mode of the design. That is the design.

Legally, the proposal argues most jurisdictions need a small carve-out: a recognised class of non-speculative, contribution-based instruments (call it a "regenerative asset") so that a community's care ledger can never be regulated — or captured — as if it were a cryptocurrency exchange. That argument has been put, in public, to at least one national parliament as a formal inquiry submission; it remains exactly that — an argument.

The ledger needs a home — sovereign compute

The legislative ask

One page of law, anywhere on Earth.

The whole ask fits in a sentence: recognise a class of non-financial, non-speculative contribution receipts — call it a regenerative asset — so a community's care ledger can never be regulated, taxed or captured as if it were a casino chip. No budget line, no new agency, no surveillance power. A definition and a carve-out.

The working draft, from the P4A papers — offered to any legislature, assembly or nation's law-making body to adopt, improve or outdo:

A Regenerative Asset (Community-Hour, C-Hour) is a digital receipt that: (a) records exactly one hour of verified human contribution to care, community wellbeing, ecological stewardship or disaster readiness, as defined by the issuing nation, community or government; (b) is earned only through that effort — never bought, sold, or offered as an investment; (c) embodies radical equality — one hour equals one C-Hour regardless of who contributed it; and (d) is governed by the community it serves, for the benefit of the community it serves.

Draft statutory definition, P4A working papers — purple register: a draft to legislate against, not settled text

Versions of this ask are already in public: a submission to an Australian federal parliamentary inquiry proposing the carve-out in corporations and digital-asset law, and research runs mapping equivalent carve-outs through United States, European, United Kingdom, Pacific and Asian frameworks. The pattern repeats everywhere: existing law only knows securities, commodities and stablecoins — the C-Hour is deliberately none of them, so every jurisdiction needs the same small door cut. First mover writes the template the rest copy.

Why native nations could move first

Ancestral fit, sovereign speed — and everyone's invited.

FitReciprocity is the ancestral system

Gift obligation, kinship duty and care-for-Country ran sophisticated societies for millennia without money at the centre. The C-Hour is the newcomer asking to be useful — a receipt printer offered to the oldest working reciprocity economies on Earth, on their terms.

SpeedSovereign law-making

Many nations legislate under their own authority. A nation's council or assembly can recognise a regenerative asset class under its own law without waiting for any settler parliament — and a working example under Indigenous law would be the strongest possible exhibit when the settler parliaments finally ask how it's done.

LeverageThe record room as crowbar

Where treaties, settlements and consultation duties already exist, reciprocity clauses can ride along on every renewal — and consent language states have already signed is what makes a parliament's door swing when the ask arrives.

EveryoneChildren of Earth, all of us

The C-Hour carries no membership and is nobody's product. In the end we are all children of Earth and the Sol System; native nations are first movers here by fit and by right, not by exclusion. Any suburb, parish, village or city that wants care counted can take the same pattern home today.

The seams in this proposal

Where it cracks, honestly.

VerificationWho says the hour happened?

Verification is the hard problem: too loose invites gaming; too tight becomes surveillance of carers — the worst possible outcome. Community-scale trust plus spot-verification is our current best sketch, and it is only a sketch.

CultureCounting can corrode

Some obligations lose their meaning the moment they are measured. Some care should never appear on any ledger, however benign. Where that line sits is cultural knowledge, not systems design — a reason nations lead and tools follow.

CaptureGovernments could co-opt it

A state could adopt C-Hours and quietly make them a condition of benefits — mobilisation inverted into obligation. Any real deployment needs constitutional-grade protections against exactly this.

ErrorsThe maths of the source papers

The economic modelling in the source documents is rapid iteration by one person with AI assistance. Treat every figure as a provocation to check, not a finding to cite.
